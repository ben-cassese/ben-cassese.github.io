Current Research

Spectral features across Io

Mapping variation in composition using UV and visible reflectance spectroscopy

When the sun shines on Io, some of that light is reflected back towards us on earth. Not all wavelengths are equally likely to bounce, though: when light interacts with a surface mineral, that mineral preferentially absorbs certain wavelengths. This means the spectrum of reflected light will have "dips" at certain wavelengths, a fingerprint of interactions with a specific mineral. By using the STIS instrument aboard the Hubble Space Telescope, we can measure these dips in the spectrum of every location on the surface. With this information, we can construct maps of minerological variation.

Changes Io's surface with time

How Io's volcanoes affect its surface composition over decades

Io is the most geologically active body in the solar system, and the only other known world with non-cryogenic volcanism. Its volcanoes are so active, large swaths of its surface are buried every couple of years. While we can image Io in the infrared to map its active volcanos and hotspots, archival analysis of visible wavelength reflectance spectroscopy allows us to track the subsequent alteration of volcanic material after settling sitting in the intense radiation environment.

Io's surface roughness

Tracking how a proxy for Io's surface roughness varies with wavelength

While we can't yet go to Io and touch its surface, we can get an idea of how rough it may be through observations. For many years, animators and video game developers have labored to create algorithms which accurately generate images of rough surfaces under different lighting conditions. We can apply the results of those efforts to the reverse problem: taking an image captured under known lighting conditions, then fitting parameters in those algorithms to estimate the surface roughness. While this is typically done via imaging, creating limb profiles at different wavelengths allows us to investigate any wavelength dependencies, another clue in total surface composition.
